## Earnings Long Straddle (Expect Big Move)

**In plain English:** The opposite of the IV crush strategy — here you BUY the straddle before earnings, expecting the actual move to be even bigger than what the market is pricing in. You profit if the stock moves dramatically in either direction. You lose if the stock barely moves (common case) or if IV collapses faster than the move compensates.

---

### When to Buy Instead of Sell

Most of the time, selling earnings vol is better (options overprice the move). But some stocks and situations consistently see LARGER moves than implied:

1. **Consistently "surprising" stocks** — companies with poor analyst coverage or opaque businesses
2. **Binary regulatory events** — FDA drug approvals, DOJ rulings
3. **First post-IPO earnings** — no track record, massive uncertainty
4. **Companies with activist investors** or pending M&A (unannounced until earnings call)
5. **Severely crowded short positions** — miss = short squeeze scenario

### Entry Checklist

- [ ] Historical implied/actual ratio for this stock < 1.0 (actual has exceeded implied more than half the time)
- [ ] Clear structural reason for potential large move (AI adoption, drug approval, major contract win)
- [ ] Options IV rank < 80% (if already above 80%, the straddle might already price in the big move)
- [ ] Enter at 3:30–4:00pm day of earnings (IV highest pre-announcement)
- [ ] Close within 30 minutes of open the next day — IV crush begins immediately

---

### Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming this works for all stocks.** For AAPL, which almost always moves less than implied (3.1% actual vs 4.8% implied), buying the straddle loses money on average. Do the historical research first.

2. **Holding too long.** After NVDA gapped 10%, you could have made 47%. If you waited 2 hours, IV crushed from 65% to 25% and your 10% move only gives you 15% profit instead.

3. **Using ATM straddles when OTM strangles would be cheaper.** If the expected move is 10%, buying a strangle at ±12% OTM costs half the premium. You give up the first ±12% of move but dramatically reduce your break-even requirement for the "blow-out" scenario.
